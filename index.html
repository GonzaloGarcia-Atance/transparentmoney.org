Transparent Money

A privacy-preserving architecture for real-time public spending transparency.

Transparent Money proposes a public information layer in which each digital cent receives a tracking number. This makes it possible to follow the movement of money through the economy without publishing the identity of private citizens or companies. The objective is simple: make public money visible to the last cent while keeping privacy protected.

What is Transparent Money?

Transparent Money is not a new currency, not a central bank digital currency, and not a payment system. It is a proposed public, structured, privacy-preserving information layer built around existing money and existing banking infrastructure.

Its central idea is that money can be traced without tracing people. If each digital cent has a tracking number, then the movement of money can be recorded by following the money itself, without recording the names of payer and payee. The public record does not need those names because money itself has identity.

The core idea

1. If each digital cent has a tracking number, money itself has identity.
2. Because money itself has identity, a record of monetary movement does not need the identity of the payer and payee.
3. Because payer and payee identity is not needed in that record, the record can be made public.
4. Public money can carry higher detail; private transactions can remain generic.
5. Where inference risks remain, individuals and firms can have the tracking numbers associated with their money automatically exchanged by their bank, receiving the same monetary value with different tracking numbers.
6. Private users do not know the tracking numbers of the money they hold, and the government does not know them either. Therefore, public money becomes visible to the last cent while private life remains protected.

Why it matters

Public spending transparency is often delayed, fragmented, technical, and difficult for ordinary citizens to understand. Transparent Money aims to transform fiscal transparency from occasional disclosure into continuous visibility. Citizens, journalists, researchers, businesses, and policymakers could see how public money is collected, transferred, and spent in real time. Because it would also provide useful information about the economy, it would not depend only on civic interest: many people would use it because it would help them make better decisions.

The wider benefit is economic. A public, privacy-preserving map of money flows could improve public accountability, reduce waste and corruption, weaken insider information advantages, and give citizens and businesses better information for decision-making. Millions of small improvements, accumulated year after year over decades, could work like compound interest for society.

Privacy by architecture

Transparent Money is designed to reverse the growing tendency to make citizens increasingly visible to the state. It proposes using technology in the opposite direction: not to monitor private life, but to make government visible, because government must be accountable to citizens. Private identities are not published in the public ledger. Private transactions use generic codes and broad descriptors, while public transactions carry higher detail because they involve public money.

Additional protections can include splitting unusual amounts into common denominations, coarsening time and location, generalising private transaction categories, and using short-term reissuance pools to exchange tracking numbers. This helps stop inference when an actor knows an external transaction pattern and might otherwise try to follow another person’s or firm’s money path.

Related fields

Transparent Money connects with several important fields: blockchain transparency, open government, fiscal transparency, public money accountability, anti-corruption technology, democratic oversight, digital money, public ledgers and distributed ledger systems. It is also relevant to debates about central bank digital currencies, cryptocurrencies and the future of public finance, although Transparent Money is not a cryptocurrency, not a CBDC and not a new payment system.

The aim is to use modern digital infrastructure to make public spending visible in real time while preserving privacy. In simple terms, Transparent Money is designed to trace money without tracing people.
